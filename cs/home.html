On Building Systems that will Fail - Fernando J. Corbato's Turing Award Speech

20 May 2017

Fernando J. Corbato was given the Turing Award, the highest honor in Computer Science for his pioneering work in organizing the concepts and leading the development of the general-purpose, large-scale, time-sharing and resource-sharing computer systems, CTSS and Multics. The Multi-Level Feedback Queue (MLFQ) CPU scheduling approach was first described in CTSS. Its a scheduler that can balance both response time and throughput without prior knowledge of process running time. It has multiple levels of processes, with the preference given to the highest-level. Processes in the same level are run in Round Robin fashion. When a process uses up its time slice, it is demoted to a lower level. Periodically, the priority of every process is boosted. This ensures attention to earlier-CPU-intensive processes that may later turn interactive.

In the speech, Corbato talks about how failure is inevitable in complex, ambitious systems. He offers six ways to resolve the problem:

  1. emphasis on elegance (achieving a given functionality with minimum mechanism and maximum clarity) and simplicity
  2. do not underestimate value of metaphors. Metaphors are a way of internalizing and abstracting concepts allowing one's thinking to be on a higher plane and low-level mistakes to be avoided.
  3. Use of constrained language for design or syntesis: by not allowing a programmer or designer to express irrelevant ideas, the domain of possible ideas becomes far more limited.
  4. Anticipate human error and hardware failure: need to attach likelihood of occurrence to events and to address issues of the independence of failures.
  5. During design, assume that the system will have to be repaired/modified.
  6. Educational redundancy of the team in large projects is a good investment.

This piece is similar to Brook's Mythical Man Month or No Silver Bullet: very management-y. Perhaps, one day I'll appreciate such works.
